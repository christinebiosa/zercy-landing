**Short answer:** Parking wins if you live near the airport, travel for only a few days, and use an economy or off-site lot. A taxi or private transfer wins if your trip is long, you would rather leave the car safely at home, or you land exhausted. Rule of thumb: with a terminal garage the math flips in favor of the transfer after about four days.

You stand there on the morning of your trip facing the same old question. Drive your own car and park at the airport? Or just call a cab and leave the car at home? Both options sound practical. On price they often sit far apart, and the difference hangs on things almost nobody thinks about first: how long you are gone, how far you live, and exactly where you park.

## How much does airport parking really cost?

The price explodes depending on the lot type. Terminal garages are the most expensive. At major US hubs you can pay 24 to 60 dollars a day on-site, and at JFK or LAX terminal garages push past 70 dollars a day. Economy lots run 15 to 40 dollars, while off-site lots with a shuttle drop to just 5 to 15 dollars a day. For those last few minutes you ride a shuttle bus to the terminal.

Off-site operators do not pay airport rents, so their daily rate often lands 30 to 60 percent below the on-airport equivalent. That gap is the single biggest lever you control. Booking online ahead of time also beats the drive-up rate almost every time.

A pattern shows up right away. Parking charges you per day. The longer you are gone, the more the bill stacks up.

## How much does a taxi or private transfer cost?

Here the logic runs the other way. A taxi or transfer costs a fixed amount per ride, whether you are gone three days or three weeks. From JFK to Midtown Manhattan a yellow cab is a 70 dollar flat fare plus tolls and tip, so figure 80 to 90 dollars one way. In London a black cab from Heathrow to the centre runs 80 to 120 pounds, an Uber 45 to 70 pounds, and a pre-booked private car 55 to 85 pounds.

A private [airport transfer through a service like Welcome Pickups](https://www.welcomepickups.com/) gives you a fixed price and a driver who meets you with a name sign. That is predictable. No queue, no meter climbing in traffic. For families with heavy bags or early departures it is often the calmest option. ## When does parking beat a taxi?

Now the math gets concrete. Take the round-trip taxi cost and divide it by the daily parking rate. That gives you the break-even.

Example: a round-trip cab of roughly 140 to 160 dollars. With off-site parking at 10 dollars a day, parking stays cheaper for about 14 to 16 days. Go longer and the cab wins. Park in a terminal garage at 40 dollars a day and the math flips after just three or four days. A weekend of parking almost always beats two cab rides. A three-week trip rarely does.

Beyond raw price sits the value of your car. Three weeks in the open sun on an exposed lot is not for everyone. If you would rather not leave the car out for that long, the transfer starts to look better.

## Parking or transfer: the decision grid

So you do not have to run numbers, here is the clear split.

Parking wins when:

- you are gone only a short time, a weekend to about a week
- you fly very early or very late, when no train or bus runs
- you travel as a group and split the cost
- you need the car right after you land
- you pre-book a cheap off-site or economy lot

Taxi or transfer wins when:

- your trip is long, from about two weeks, because parking adds up daily
- you would rather leave the car safely at home
- you live close enough that the fixed cab fare stays low
- you travel with heavy bags or small kids and comfort matters
- you land drained after a long-haul flight and want to be driven

## What about park and ride as a third option?

There is a third path many people forget. Park and ride. You park cheaply or free a bit outside the city and take a train or metro to the airport. Public transit is almost always the cheapest way in, often a few dollars each way instead of a full cab fare or a week of parking. The [airport bus and shuttle network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airport_bus) covers most major hubs and connects to rental and hotel lots too.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much cheaper is off-site parking than a terminal garage?

Off-site lots often run 5 to 15 dollars a day, while terminal garages charge 24 to 60 dollars or more. Over a week that gap can top 200 dollars. You pay for it with a short shuttle ride to the terminal.

### When does a private transfer beat a taxi?

A private transfer beats a taxi when you want a fixed price, fly early or late, carry heavy bags, or want to be met after a long flight. The fare is locked in ahead of time and does not climb in traffic.

### What does long-term airport parking cost in the US?

A week of off-site parking often starts around 50 to 90 dollars with a shuttle. A terminal garage can pass 200 dollars for the same week. Booking online ahead is almost always cheaper than the drive-up rate.

### Why is public transport often the cheapest way to the airport?

Public transport pairs cheap or free parking on the edge of town with a low-cost train or bus. It usually beats both parking and a cab on price. It only loses ground when you have heavy bags or travel at awkward hours.
