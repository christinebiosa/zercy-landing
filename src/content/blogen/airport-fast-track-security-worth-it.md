> **Short answer:** Fast Track is worth it when your connection is tight, you fly on a peak day, or you travel with kids. For roughly 50 to 200 USD per person you typically save 30 to 60 minutes. But with a big time buffer or off-peak travel, it is wasted money. The short wait is never guaranteed.

It is Friday afternoon, the start of the holidays, and the security line snakes three times around the barriers. Ahead of you: what feels like 200 people, laptops, crying kids, someone who cannot find their belt. The screen says 45 minutes. Your connecting flight leaves in 70. You do the math in your head and you do not like the result.

And then, two meters to your left, that nearly empty lane. A sign: Fast Track. Three people standing there, through in two minutes. You ask yourself: should I just have booked the 30-dollar pass beforehand?

That is exactly the question we settle here. With real numbers and a clear verdict.

## What is Fast Track, really?

Fast Track is a paid express lane through security. You pay a fee, and in return you skip the regular line and go to your own, less crowded checkpoint. That is all it is. The screening itself runs the same way. You only save the wait before it.

Important: do not confuse Fast Track with two other things.

It is not the same as [Global Entry or TSA PreCheck](/en/blog/global-entry-tsa-precheck-worth-it/). Those are US trusted traveler programs with a background check and membership. Fast Track you simply book for a single flight.

And it is not a [Priority Pass](/en/blog/priority-pass-worth-it/) either. That gets you into the lounge, not through security.

## What does Fast Track actually cost?

Prices swing a lot by airport and time of day. Roughly, you are looking at 50 to 200 USD per person. Add a personal escort, someone who walks you from check-in to the gate, and it can climb to 300 USD or more.

A concrete example from London Heathrow, one of the few airports that publish their prices openly. According to the official [Heathrow Fast Track page](https://www.heathrow.com/at-the-airport/airport-services/fast-track), the departure lane starts at 12.99 pounds per person. For arrivals, the passport control Fast Track costs 25 pounds off-peak and 35 pounds at peak times.

That shows the pattern. Book early and at a quiet time, and it is cheap. Buy on the spot on a peak day, and it gets expensive. Exactly when you need it most, it costs the most.

## How much time does Fast Track save?

Realistically you save 30 to 60 minutes. On extreme days, the start of the holidays or during a disruption, it can be up to 90 minutes. On a quiet Tuesday morning at a small airport, you might save five minutes. Then you paid for nothing.

One point many people miss: the wait is not guaranteed. When demand is high or there are technical problems, some airports simply suspend Fast Track. No airport promises you a maximum wait time, not even in the paid lane. You are buying a better chance, not a guarantee.

## Can I get Fast Track for free?

Often yes, and that is the first thing to check before you spend money.

In **business or first class** Fast Track is almost always included in the ticket. Look at your boarding pass, it is often printed right on it.

With **frequent flyer status** you usually get it too. Star Alliance Gold, for example, opens the so-called Gold Track lane. The same goes for oneworld and SkyTeam at higher tiers.

Some **premium credit cards** include Fast Track as a travel benefit. Read the fine print on your card before you pay extra.

If any of these apply to you, the whole cost question is settled. You just walk through.

## When is it worth it, and when not?

Here is the decision grid, no fluff.

**Worth it when:**

- Your connection is tight and every minute counts.
- You fly on a peak day or at the start of the holidays.
- You travel with kids and want to avoid the stress.
- You have flight anxiety and do not want to rush.
- You value your time highly and happily pay 30 dollars for an hour.

**Not worth it when:**

- You fly off-peak or at a small airport.
- You have a big time buffer and arrive early.
- You already have business class or status with Fast Track included.
- You would only save a few minutes and would rather spend the money on coffee at the gate.

## A worked example: a family of four

Take a family of four, flying out on the first day of the holidays. Fast Track costs 20 euros per person here, so 80 euros for all of them. In return, you save about 50 minutes of waiting on this day with two whiny kids.

Worth it? If your connection is tight or you would otherwise be the last in an endless line: yes, clearly. 80 euros for a relaxed start to the trip instead of 50 minutes of stress with the whole family. That is a fair deal.

But if the same family travels on a quiet Wednesday with a three-hour buffer, those 80 euros are wasted. You would be at the gate in ten minutes anyway. The context decides, not the price alone.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between Fast Track and TSA PreCheck?

Fast Track is a paid express lane you book per flight, with no registration or check. TSA PreCheck is a US program with a background check and membership. With PreCheck you also keep your laptop and shoes on, while Fast Track runs the same screening as the regular lane.

### How much does Fast Track cost on average?

You usually pay 50 to 200 USD per person, depending on the airport and time of day. At Heathrow the departure lane starts at just 12.99 pounds. With a personal escort service it can reach 300 USD or more.

### Why is my wait long despite Fast Track?

Because no airport guarantees a maximum wait time. When demand is very high or there are disruptions, Fast Track is sometimes suspended or busy itself. You buy a better chance at a short line, not a firm promise.

### When is Fast Track worth it for a family?

When you fly on a peak day, your connection is tight, or you want to avoid stress with kids. For four people at 20 euros each you pay 80 euros for about 50 saved minutes. On a quiet day with plenty of buffer it is not worth it.
