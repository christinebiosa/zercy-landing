> **Short answer:** Yes, for almost every traveler a dedicated travel card is worth it. A fee-free card easily saves you $40 to $80 in hidden charges on a longer trip. The ideal setup is a no-foreign-transaction-fee travel credit card for purchases plus a debit card like Charles Schwab or Wise for cash.

You're at an ATM in Lisbon. You withdraw 200 euros and the screen cheerfully offers you a "convenient" exchange rate. You tap accept. Three weeks later your statement shows the truth: a 3% foreign transaction fee from your bank, a $5 ATM fee, and a hidden currency markup that quietly added another few dollars. One small cash withdrawal, eight dollars gone. And that was just the first ATM of the trip.

So here's the real question. Do you actually need a dedicated travel money card, or is your everyday bank card fine? Let's run the numbers honestly, with the cards US, UK and global travelers can actually get in 2026.

## What Do Normal Bank Cards Really Cost Abroad?

Most standard US bank cards add roughly 3% in foreign transaction fees on every purchase made in another currency. On top of that come ATM fees: a flat charge from your own bank, plus a fee from the local ATM operator, plus sometimes a sneaky dynamic currency conversion markup if you accept the machine's "home currency" offer.

These feel like small numbers. They are not. Spend $1,500 on a three-week trip and pull cash four times, and you can easily lose $60 to $80 to fees alone. Money that simply evaporates across your statement, line by line.

Charles Schwab, whose debit card is a US travelers' favorite, [lays out exactly how these costs stack up and how to avoid them](https://www.schwab.com/learn/story/ways-to-save-money-when-traveling-abroad). The good news: there are genuinely fee-free alternatives.

## Which Travel Card Is Worth It?

It helps to split travel cards into two jobs, because the best options differ depending on whether you need cash or you're paying by card.

For cash withdrawals, the standout is the Charles Schwab Bank debit card: $0 foreign transaction fees and unlimited worldwide ATM fee reimbursement, with no cap. [Wise](https://wise.com/) gives you the mid-market rate across 40+ currencies with a small, clearly shown conversion fee of 0.35 to 1%, plus a true multi-currency account. Revolut offers $0 FX, lets you hold 30+ currencies, and gives free ATM withdrawals up to around $200 a month before a 2% fee kicks in, with solid budgeting tools. Other strong options include SoFi, Chime, Varo and Capital One ($0 FX). In the UK, look at Monzo, Starling, Chase UK and Revolut.

For everyday purchases, a no-FX travel credit card is your friend: rewards on spending, the ability to cover rental and hotel deposits, and built-in travel insurance and purchase protection.

## Credit or Debit? You Want Both

This is where a lot of travelers get it wrong. The smartest setup is not one card, it's two.

Use a no-foreign-transaction-fee travel credit card for purchases. It earns rewards, it covers rental car and hotel deposits (which almost always require a credit card, not debit), and it often bundles trip insurance. Then carry a fee-friendly debit card, Schwab or Wise, for pulling local cash from ATMs without bleeding fees.

Carrying both also protects you. If one card is lost, frozen by your bank's fraud system, or eaten by an ATM, you're not stranded. Before you book the car, run through our [rental car checklist](/en/blog/rental-car-checklist/) so the deposit card situation doesn't catch you out at the counter.

## Worked Example: A Three-Week Trip

Say you spend $2,000 by card on a long trip and withdraw cash three times, $200 each.

With a normal bank card: $2,000 times a 3% foreign transaction fee is $60. Add three withdrawals at $5 bank fee each, plus roughly $3.50 in local ATM fees per pull, and that's another $25 or so. Total: about $85 in fees you barely notice because they're scattered across the statement.

With fee-free travel cards: paying on a no-FX credit card costs $0. Pulling cash on a Schwab card means the ATM fees get reimbursed at month's end, so you pay close to nothing, and Wise charges only a small visible conversion fee. Total: roughly $0 to $10.

Savings per trip: around $75. Take two trips a year and you've kept $150 in your pocket, while giving up nothing.

## When Is It NOT Worth It?

Honesty matters. If you almost never leave your home currency zone, there's no foreign transaction fee to dodge, so the upside shrinks to ATM fees only. If you travel once every few years and pay mostly in cash, the savings are modest. And if you already carry a no-FX credit card, you may not need a third card at all.

## Decision Grid

**Worth it if:**

- You travel abroad outside your home currency
- You withdraw cash overseas or spend a lot on card
- You rent a car or book hotels that require a deposit
- You want a backup card for emergencies

**Not worth it if:**

- You rarely leave your home currency zone
- You travel rarely and pay almost everything in cash
- You already use a no-foreign-transaction-fee credit card

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between a travel money card and my normal bank card?

A travel card skips the foreign transaction fee and converts at the real mid-market rate. Your normal bank card typically adds about 3% on every purchase in another currency, plus ATM fees. Over a longer trip that gap easily reaches $60 or more.

### When do I absolutely need a credit card instead of a debit card?

Whenever a deposit needs to be held. Rental car companies and many hotels require a real credit card with a credit limit to authorize a hold. Debit cards, including Schwab and Wise, are often rejected for this. So always travel with both types.

### How many cards should I bring on a trip?

At least two different cards from different providers. If one gets frozen, lost or swallowed by an ATM, you have a backup. The ideal pairing is a no-FX travel credit card plus a fee-friendly debit card like Schwab or Wise.

### Which card is best for pulling cash abroad?

For US travelers, the Charles Schwab debit card is hard to beat: $0 foreign transaction fees and unlimited worldwide ATM fee reimbursement. Wise is the best globally available alternative thanks to the real mid-market rate and a small, transparent conversion fee.
