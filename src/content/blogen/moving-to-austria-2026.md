**Short answer:** If you hold an EU, EEA or Swiss passport, you can move to Austria without a visa. If you are American or British, Brexit ended free movement for UK citizens, so you now need the points-based Red-White-Red Card like any other third-country national. Everyone registers their address within three days of moving in. Budget around 1,700 euros a month in Vienna, less in rural areas.

Austria ranked fourth for quality of life in the InterNations 2025 survey. Low crime, punctual public transport, strong healthcare and the Alps on your doorstep make it a magnet for expats who want order and nature. But the paperwork depends heavily on where your passport is from.

This guide shows how the move really works in 2026. For US and UK readers it centers on the Red-White-Red Card. We also cover registration, real costs and how to get into the public health system.

## Who can move to Austria the easy way?

Citizens of the EU, the EEA or Switzerland enjoy full free movement. No visa, no residence permit, no quota. They can live and work from day one. For everyone else, including Americans and, since Brexit, British citizens, the path runs through a residence permit.

That is the key split. A German or a Frenchman just registers. A US or UK national has to qualify for a permit before working. If you are weighing several destinations, our ranking of the [best countries for expat quality of life](/en/blog/best-countries-expat-quality-of-life-2026/) puts Austria in context against Spain, Portugal and the rest.

The good news: Austria actively recruits skilled workers. If your job or qualifications fit, the Red-White-Red Card is a well-defined route rather than a lottery.

## EU citizen or third-country national: what applies to you?

The whole process hinges on your nationality. Use this framework:

- **You hold an EU, EEA or Swiss passport:** No visa needed. Register your address within three days, apply for a registration certificate if you stay over three months. Work allowed immediately.
- **You are a third-country national (US, UK post-Brexit, most of Latin America):** You need a residence permit, usually the Red-White-Red Card. It is points-based, sometimes quota-linked and normally tied to a specific employer.
- **You are the family member of an EU citizen:** You often benefit from easier rules even as a third-country national. Details depend on your case.

For American and British readers, the second point is your reality. Brexit removed the automatic right that UK citizens once had, so a British move now looks much like an American one.

## How does the Red-White-Red Card work?

The card serves seven categories, including Very Highly Qualified Workers, Skilled Workers in Shortage Occupations, Other Key Workers, university graduates and start-up founders. A points system scores your education, work experience, age and language skills. Very highly qualified applicants need at least 70 of 100 points. Skilled workers in shortage occupations and other key workers need at least 55 points, usually with a minimum salary attached.

The card is issued for 24 months and tied to the employer named in your application. On top of the points, you must show a fixed income covering your living costs (around 1,308 euros a month for a single person in 2026), health insurance valid in Austria, and no threat to public order. A points calculator on the official portal gives you a first read. For the full rules, check the [Austrian migration portal](https://www.migration.gv.at/en/types-of-immigration/permanent-immigration/) before you apply, because thresholds change each year. ## How do you register once you arrive?

Everyone who lives in Austria has to register their address. Within three days of moving into a flat, you file the Meldezettel at the local registration office. It is free. You need your ID and a signature from your landlord or host.

EU, EEA and Swiss citizens who stay longer than three months also apply for a registration certificate (Anmeldebescheinigung) at the district authority, within four months of arrival, for a fee of around 15 euros. Third-country nationals do not use this step. They hold the residence card itself, which already proves their right to stay.

Get the address registration done early. Without it, opening a bank account, signing a phone contract or enrolling in health insurance gets harder. Where to base yourself in the capital while you hunt for a flat is covered in our guide to [where to stay in Vienna](/en/blog/where-to-stay-vienna/).

## What does living in Austria cost?

Vienna is the priciest spot but moderate by Western European standards. Budget about 1,700 euros a month for one person including rent. A one-bedroom flat runs around 850 euros in the center and closer to 590 euros in the outer districts. Groceries add 400 to 500 euros, and the annual public transport pass costs 365 euros, one euro a day.

Rural Austria is noticeably cheaper. A small flat outside Vienna often goes for 600 to 800 euros. The gap between a big city and the countryside can reach 900 euros a month, for example between Innsbruck and rural Burgenland. Styria, Carinthia and the Waldviertel region offer the biggest savings if you can work remotely or commute.

## How does health insurance work?

Austria runs a mandatory public health system. Employees are automatically covered by the Austrian Health Insurance Fund (ÖGK), with contributions taken from salary and the employer handling enrollment. The self-employed usually fall under the SVS fund. Either way you get full access to doctors, hospitals and prescriptions.

If you are not employed, you can insure yourself voluntarily. The general voluntary health insurance costs around 565 euros a month in 2026, though it can be reduced based on income. Students pay a much lower rate of about 79 euros. Remember that a Red-White-Red Card application already requires valid health cover, so line this up before you file.

## Why is quality of life so high?

Austria sits near the top of livability rankings for solid reasons. Public safety is strong and crime is low. Trains and trams run on time and cover the country densely, so in Vienna you rarely need a car. Healthcare is excellent and broadly accessible for the insured.

Then there is the nature. Mountains, lakes and hiking trails are never far, whether you live in Innsbruck, Salzburg or the capital. In winter the nearest ski lift is often a short train ride away. Vienna repeatedly tops global most-liveable-city lists thanks to its parks, culture and coffee houses. If you want warm winters instead, [Portugal](/en/blog/moving-to-portugal-2026/) or [Spain](/en/blog/moving-to-spain-2026/) fit better. If you want structure, safety and the Alps, Austria delivers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Who needs a Red-White-Red Card for Austria?
Third-country nationals, meaning people from outside the EU, EEA and Switzerland, generally need it to live and work in Austria. Since Brexit that includes UK citizens, alongside Americans and most Latin Americans. A points system scores qualifications, experience, age and language skills.

### How quickly do I have to register my address?
You must file the Meldezettel within three days of moving into a home, at the local registration office, and it is free. EU, EEA and Swiss citizens staying over three months also apply for a registration certificate within four months of arrival.

### What does living in Vienna cost per month?
Budget around 1,700 euros for one person including rent. A one-bedroom flat ranges from about 590 euros in the outskirts to 850 euros in the center. Rural areas are cheaper, often 600 to 800 euros for a small flat, with savings of up to 900 euros a month.

### How do I join the Austrian health system?
Employees are enrolled automatically in the ÖGK by their employer. The self-employed usually use the SVS fund. If you are not working, you can take voluntary insurance for around 565 euros a month in 2026, an amount that can be lowered based on your income.
