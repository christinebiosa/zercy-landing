**Short answer:** Full-to-full is almost always the cheapest choice. You pay only for the fuel you actually burn, then top up the tank yourself before you hand the car back. Prepaid fuel is usually a rip-off: you buy a whole tank upfront and get nothing back for the fuel you leave behind. Decline it.

You are at the counter, the flight was long, and the agent smiles: "Want to add the full tank now? Just 75 dollars upfront, then you drop the car off empty. Much more relaxed, no hunting for a gas station before your flight." It sounds convenient. It sounds fair. It rarely is. This sales pitch costs you real money in most cases, and it lands exactly when you have the least energy to do the math. Let's break down why.

## What does full-to-full mean versus prepaid?

There are two basic models, and the difference decides your final bill.

**Full-to-full** means you pick the car up with a full tank and return it with a full tank. You pay only for the fuel you use, and at normal pump prices. Right before drop-off you swing by a gas station, fill up, done. This is the model that costs you the least.

**Prepaid** (buy full, return empty) flips the logic: you pay for a complete tank at pickup and then return the car empty. The catch is that fuel you don't burn is never refunded. Not even if you arrive with a half-full tank. Whatever is left in the tank is money you have lost.

## Why is prepaid usually a losing deal?

Because the math almost never works in your favor. The break-even point only arrives when you return the car with roughly five percent left in the tank. Timing that is practically impossible. Nobody drives a rental down to the fumes and plans it out over the last miles to the airport. In more than 85 percent of cases, filling up yourself is cheaper.

Rental companies know this exactly. They build their profit straight from the fuel you leave behind. So you are not paying for fair value, you are paying for a bit of flexibility you usually don't need. The travel experts at [Frommers](https://www.frommers.com/tips/car-bus-rail/once-and-for-all-should-you-pre-pay-for-fuel-when-you-rent-a-car) also recommend full-to-full and declining prepaid fuel, as long as you can refuel before returning the car.

## What does not filling up cost under full-to-full?

Full-to-full has one trap: if you don't return the car completely full, the refueling fee kicks in. And it stings. The company tops up the tank for you, but at their price, not the pump price.

In practice that runs between 2 and 4 times the normal fuel price. The cheapest lately was Thrifty at around 8.50 dollars per gallon, which is 2.3 times pump price. The priciest was Sixt at around 12.50 dollars per gallon, so 3.4 times. A small bit of laziness turns into a fat surcharge fast.

So the rule is simple: with full-to-full, always fill up yourself right before drop-off, and keep the gas station receipt. That ends any argument. For how to slot this cleanly into your return routine, see our [rental car checklist](/en/blog/rental-car-checklist/).

## When does prepaid actually make sense?

There are situations where prepaid fuel is the more relaxed choice. Not the cheapest, but the better fit.

**Worth it if:**

- You will return the car almost empty because you drive a lot right up to the end
- Your flight is early and you have no time for a gas station detour
- The drop-off location is remote with no station nearby
- You are happy to pay a few dollars more to save time

**Not worth it if:**

- You only drive a short or medium distance and a lot of fuel is left over
- There are plenty of gas stations around the drop-off point
- You have time flexibility and can easily plan the detour
- You have a large car with a large tank, because that is where the markup hits hardest

## A worked example that settles it

Take a car with a 50-liter tank and a one-week short trip. You burn about half, so 25 liters.

Full-to-full: at the end you refill the 25 liters you used, at 1.80 per liter. That's 45. You pay nothing more.

Prepaid: you buy the full 50-liter tank upfront for 75. The 25 liters you don't burn are gone. Not a cent back.

Difference: 30 that you simply hand over with prepaid. For nothing. And that was a moderate example. With a bigger tank or fewer miles driven, the gap grows even wider.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is cheaper, full-to-full or prepaid?

Full-to-full is cheaper in more than 85 percent of cases because you pay only for the fuel you burn at pump prices. With prepaid, you get no refund for the fuel you leave in the tank.

### How much is the refueling fee?

It runs between 2 and 4 times the normal fuel price. The cheapest lately was Thrifty at 2.3 times, the priciest Sixt at 3.4 times. That is why, with full-to-full, you should always fill up yourself.

### When is prepaid fuel worth it as an exception?

When you return the car almost empty, your flight is early, the drop-off spot is remote, or there is no gas station nearby. Then you are paying for time saved, not for value.

### Where do I pick the fuel policy?

Ideally at the time of booking. Many providers show the fuel policy upfront, so you can choose full-to-full on purpose. At the counter you simply decline the prepaid offer.
