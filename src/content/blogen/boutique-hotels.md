There are hotels where you sleep. And there are hotels you're still talking about years later.

The second kind is almost always small, independent, and full of personality.

## What Makes a Boutique Hotel

No official definition. But the common traits are clear:

- Few rooms (usually under 50)
- Individual design, not a chain template
- Local owners or a small engaged team
- Personal service, not a call center
- Often in historic buildings, converted villas, old city palaces

The difference from a chain hotel: ask for a restaurant recommendation at 10pm and you won't get a TripAdvisor list. You'll get a real one.

## Why They're Worth It

**Service that remembers you**
In a 40-room hotel, they know how you take your coffee by day two. In a 400-room hotel, you're room 318.

**Design with a story**
Many boutique hotels were built with real care. Original tiles, handmade furniture, works by local artists. The room isn't a product — it's an experience.

**Location that surprises**
Boutique hotels often sit in neighborhoods big chains avoid. Which is exactly where the real life of a city happens.

## Where to Find Them

Best platforms for boutique hotels:
- **Mr & Mrs Smith** — curated selection, often with exclusive perks
- **Design Hotels** — focus on architecture and interiors
- **Booking.com** — filter "Boutique Hotel" under property type

Also worth trying: book direct. Boutique hotels often give better rooms, a free upgrade, or late checkout to direct bookers — because they don't pay commission.

## When a Chain Hotel Is the Better Call

Boutique isn't always the answer. Business trips where loyalty points matter. Family trips with multiple rooms. Destinations where there simply aren't good small alternatives — the chain is the pragmatic choice.

But for a trip you want to remember?

A small hotel with character beats every Marriott.
