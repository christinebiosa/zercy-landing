There are destinations you visit. And there are ones you experience.

Santiago de Compostela belongs to the second category. Not because of the architecture. Not because of the food. But because of what happens before you arrive.

## The journey is the destination — really

The Camino de Santiago isn't a single path. There are dozens of routes, all ending at the cathedral in Santiago.

**Camino Francés** is the classic. 780 kilometers from Saint-Jean-Pied-de-Port in France across northern Spain. Four to six weeks on foot. The most famous, most popular, most walked route.

**Camino Portugués** starts in Lisbon or Porto. The route from Porto is only 240 kilometers — doable in 10–12 days. Popular with those who don't have a month to spare.

**Camino del Norte** runs along the northern coast. Wilder, more beautiful, quieter. Not for beginners, but perfect for those seeking real solitude.

Every route is different. What they share: the cathedral at the end. And the feeling that you actually did something.

## What makes the Camino special

You don't walk alone. You walk with a hundred people all heading the same direction. And after a few days, you know them all.

The retired teacher from Germany doing it for the third time. The Australian who quit his job. The couple looking for a fresh start.

The Camino attracts people who need a pause. A break. An answer to a question they may not even be able to articulate yet.

Many find it. Some don't. But almost everyone says the same thing: it was right to go.

## Santiago de Compostela itself

The city is small. Around 100,000 inhabitants. But the old town is one of the most beautiful medieval cityscapes in Europe.

**The Cathedral** is the center — a Romanesque-Baroque masterpiece that has welcomed pilgrims for centuries. The Pilgrim Mass at noon is crowded, emotional, and somehow moving even for non-religious visitors.

**Mercado de Abastos** is the city market. Fresh fish, Galician cheeses, Polbo á feira (Galician-style octopus). Go in the morning when the fishermen are still there.

**Tarta de Santiago** — almond cake dusted with a powdered-sugar pilgrim's cross — is everywhere. Eat it once, warm.

## Santiago without the Camino

You don't have to walk the route to experience the city. Santiago is worth the trip on its own.

Fly into Santiago de Compostela (SCQ) or A Coruña (LCG, one hour away). A long weekend is enough for the old town. A week is enough for the region of Galicia.
