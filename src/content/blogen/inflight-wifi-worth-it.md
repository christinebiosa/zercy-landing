**Short answer:** Check your own airline first, because inflight WiFi is increasingly free. On United, JetBlue and Delta you pay nothing, sometimes only after a free frequent flyer sign-up. If you do have to pay, it is only worth it for real work, long flights or when you need to stay reachable. For a vacation flight, load everything offline instead.

Ten hours over the Atlantic, the laptop opens, and the screen asks: buy WiFi for 19 US dollars? Your finger hovers over the confirm button. Is it worth it for a few emails and one Netflix episode? Or is it money down the drain because the connection will stutter anyway? A few years ago that question had a simple yes or no answer. Today it is more interesting, because on more and more airlines the screen suddenly says free.

## What does inflight WiFi actually cost?

On flights with older technology, you still pay. Prices are tiered, so look closely at what you get.

- **Basic pass:** from around 5 US dollars. You can browse and message, but no streaming.
- **Full pass:** roughly 10 to 30 US dollars depending on the airline. This adds streaming, often with unlimited data for the whole flight.

One detail gets overlooked a lot: messaging through WhatsApp or iMessage is free on many airlines, even when full access costs money. So if you only want to let someone know the flight is on time, you may not need to buy anything at all.

## Which airlines offer free WiFi?

This is exactly where the big shift is happening. The trend clearly points toward free WiFi, and US airlines are leading.

- **United:** upgrading to Starlink, free for every passenger regardless of cabin or status. Around 1,000 aircraft are due to fly with it by the end of 2026.
- **JetBlue:** has offered free onboard WiFi for everyone through FlyFi for years.
- **Delta:** Delta Sync is free, you just need a free SkyMiles membership that you can even sign up for during the flight. According to the [official Delta page](https://www.delta.com/us/en/onboard/inflight-entertainment/onboard-wifi), it is available on most domestic and international flights, with roughly three out of four jets equipped.

Among international airlines the picture is mixed. Emirates, for example, offers free messaging, while many European budget carriers have no WiFi at all. If you fly a lot, our [long-haul flight comfort guide](/en/blog/long-haul-flight-comfort-guide/) is worth a read before you go.

## How good is the connection really?

Free does not automatically mean fast. Quality depends heavily on the installed system.

Starlink satellite WiFi is the new benchmark. A page loads almost like at home, video calls work, streaming runs smoothly. Older systems like Gogo or ViaSat are a different world. Loading a single page can be painfully slow, and over oceans or remote areas the connection often drops entirely. Pay for a system like that and you may be paying for frustration. So the rule is simple: check which technology your aircraft has first, then decide.

## When is paying worth it, and when is it not?

If your airline has no free WiFi, it comes down to your flight and your purpose. Here is the clear grid.

**Worth it if:**

- You have real work to do and a deadline is looming.
- The flight is long and the lost hours actually matter to you.
- You need to stay reachable, for family or an important project.
- The full pass is unlimited and you will genuinely use it.

**Not worth it if:**

- It is a short flight you can easily ride out offline.
- You are heading on vacation and want to switch off anyway.
- Your plane only has an old, slow system.
- You loaded everything offline beforehand and miss nothing.

## The math: 19 dollars or load it beforehand?

Do the honest math. A streaming WiFi pass for 19 US dollars on one flight sounds harmless, but on a round trip that is already 38 US dollars. Fly six times a year and it adds up to over 200 US dollars, just to watch shows on the plane.

The alternative costs nothing but ten minutes of prep. Download your films and series offline on Netflix or Disney+ before you leave. Save your route in Google Maps for offline use. Pull a few podcasts and playlists onto your phone. That keeps you covered on a long-haul flight without spending a cent on WiFi. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does inflight WiFi cost on average?

Where you still pay, a basic pass for browsing and messaging starts at around 5 US dollars. Full passes with streaming run roughly 10 to 30 US dollars depending on the airline. On United, JetBlue and Delta, however, WiFi is already free.

### How fast is inflight WiFi really?

That depends heavily on the system. Starlink satellite WiFi is fast enough for video calls and streaming. Older systems like Gogo or ViaSat are much slower and often drop out entirely over oceans.

### When is paying for WiFi worth it?

Paying is mainly worth it for real work with a deadline, on long flights, and when you need to stay reachable. For short flights or vacations, you are better off loading everything offline and saving the money.

### Why is WiFi free on some airlines?

Airlines like United and Delta use free WiFi as a deliberate competitive edge and sometimes tie it to their free frequent flyer program. New satellite technology like Starlink makes it cheaper for them, so they pass the benefit on to passengers.
