**Short answer:** Mexico is one of the most popular relocation targets for Americans and Canadians in 2026, thanks to proximity, low costs and a huge established expat scene. Two main paths exist: the **Temporary Resident Visa** with stable income of roughly $4,300 to $4,500 per month over the last 6 months OR savings of about $73,000 held for 12 months, and the **Permanent Resident Visa** with higher thresholds, after four years as a temporary resident, or directly as a retiree. The process always starts at a Mexican consulate in your home country, followed by the canje at INM within 30 days of entry. Numbers vary by consulate and exchange rate, so always confirm them with your specific consulate.

For many people in the US and Canada, Mexico is the dream of a warm restart with rich culture, great food and lower living costs, all a short flight from home. This guide explains both key visas, the exact process, real costs, the healthcare system and the regions where expats actually live. And it stays honest about safety.

## Why do so many people move to Mexico?

Mexico is large, diverse and surprisingly well built out. Around 130 million people live between the Pacific and the Caribbean, alongside colonial cities, volcanoes, jungle and long beaches. It has one of Latin America's biggest economies and a dense network of clinics, international airports and fast internet in the larger cities.

The real magnet is the mix of price and quality of life. Your money goes further than at home without giving up good restaurants, modern apartments or fast doctors. The official language is Spanish, but in expat zones you often get by in English too. You pay in pesos. ## How does the Temporary Resident Visa work?

The Temporary Resident Visa is the standard route for most expats. It is valid for one year at first and can be renewed up to four years. You qualify through one of two paths, and each must stand on its own. You cannot combine partial amounts.

The **income path** requires stable, recurring net income of roughly $4,300 to $4,500 per month over the last six months. That can be salary, pension or regular investment income, proven with six consecutive bank statements. The **savings path** instead requires a balance of about $73,000 to $74,000 held continuously at or above that threshold for twelve months. Be careful: many consulates read the twelve-month rule strictly, and even a short dip can reset the clock.

Important for 2026: thresholds are now calculated against the UMA (Unidad de Medida y Actualizacion) rather than the minimum wage, which makes them more predictable year to year. Even so, figures vary by consulate by up to ten percent. Each dependent you add requires additional income proof of around $1,400 per month.

## Permanent Resident: for retirees and after four years

The Permanent Resident Visa gives you an open-ended stay with no annual renewal. In 2026, three routes lead there.

The first is the **retiree route**. Only retirees and pensioners can now apply for permanent residency directly from abroad in 2026. You prove a stable pension, usually over six months of statements. The thresholds sit clearly higher than the temporary visa, roughly $7,000 in monthly income or about $290,000 in savings depending on consulate and exchange rate. Some consulates quote lower figures. Never rely on rounded numbers from the web, pull the threshold from your consulate in the week you book your appointment.

The second route is time based: after **four years as a Temporary Resident** you can switch to permanent status. The third is family based, through marriage or children with Mexican citizenship. ## Which visa is right for you?

The choice depends less on the destination than on your income source and your plans. These rules of thumb help.

Choose the **Temporary Resident Visa via savings** if you have solid capital but no high recurring income. The savings path is the easiest proof for many, because an account near $73,000 is simpler to document than six months of high income.

Choose the **Temporary Resident Visa via income** if you have a good pension, a salary or stable investment returns. You stay flexible, can switch to permanent status later, and you do not tie up a large lump sum.

Choose the **Permanent Resident Visa directly** if you are a retiree who meets the higher thresholds. You skip the annual renewals and get an open-ended status right away.

A tip from experience: nearly all expats work with a local facilitator or attorney. The process is two-stage and formal, and a good helper saves stress and mistakes. The official steps are laid out by the [Instituto Nacional de Migracion (INM)](https://www.gob.mx/inm), Mexico's immigration authority.

## What does the process actually look like?

The process has two fixed stages, and the order is mandatory.

First you apply at the **Mexican consulate in your home country**. You submit bank statements, passport, photos and forms, then attend an interview. If approved, the consulate places a visa sticker in your passport, valid for 180 days for entry.

Then you fly to Mexico. At the airport you explicitly tell the officer you are entering as a resident, not a tourist. You get a stamp, and from that date you have **30 calendar days for the canje** at INM. The canje is the exchange: you create an online INM account, fill out the formato basico, pay the fee at a bank and book an appointment. At the appointment they take your photo and fingerprints, you get your CURP, and the physical resident card follows within days to two weeks. Miss the 30 days and the visa becomes void, forcing a fresh start. ## What does living in Mexico really cost?

Mexico is affordable, but the range is huge. For a couple, roughly $1,800 to $2,800 per month buys a comfortable lifestyle depending on where you land. Mexico City and the Caribbean tourist towns sit higher, while Merida or Lake Chapala run clearly lower.

Cheap wins include local food, fresh produce, household help and many services. Costs climb for imported brands, electronics and cars. Air conditioning in the hot south can also add up on your electric bill. Plan for that and you avoid nasty surprises. ## Where do most expats live?

Six regions cover nearly every profile.

**Mexico City** with the Roma and Condesa neighborhoods draws nomads and creatives: leafy streets, cafes, fast internet and a huge cultural scene. **Playa del Carmen and Tulum** on the Caribbean mean beach, an international community and higher prices. **Merida** in Yucatan is one of Mexico's safest cities, calm and colonial, ideal for families and retirees.

**Puerto Vallarta** on the Pacific pairs beach, good clinics and a large, open expat scene. **San Miguel de Allende** in the highlands is the classic artist and retiree town with mild weather. And **Lake Chapala with Ajijic** near Guadalajara has been Latin America's biggest retiree colony for decades: mild, cheap and well connected.

## Healthcare, safety and the honest reality

Healthcare is a big plus. Legal residents can voluntarily enroll in the public **IMSS** system, with an age-based annual premium of roughly $450 to $1,200. There are waiting periods and exclusions for pre-existing conditions, though. So most expats do this: pay cash for routine care, which is cheap, and add either private insurance or IMSS as an emergency backstop for hospital stays. Private policies run roughly $520 to $1,230 per person per year, more with age. In 2026 private premiums are climbing noticeably due to a new tax.

Safety deserves honesty. Mexico is highly regional. Some states have serious violence, others are very safe. Yucatan with Merida has the country's lowest homicide rate, around two per 100,000, comparable to Canada. The US State Department rates Yucatan as Level 1, the lowest advisory, on par with Japan or Iceland. The rule is simple: choose your region deliberately, ask locals, and avoid border regions with high advisories. Choose well and Mexico is a safe, rewarding place to live.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much income do I need for the Temporary Resident Visa?
In 2026 you need roughly $4,300 to $4,500 in stable net monthly income over the last six months OR about $73,000 in savings held for twelve months. Each path must stand on its own, since partial amounts cannot be combined. Figures vary by consulate by up to ten percent, so confirm them directly with your consulate.

### When must I complete the canje at INM?
Within 30 calendar days of entering Mexico. The clock starts with the stamp in your passport at the airport. You create an INM account, pay the fee and book an appointment. Miss the 30 days and the visa becomes void, forcing you to restart the whole application from a consulate abroad.

### Where do expats live most safely in Mexico?
Merida in Yucatan is one of Mexico's safest cities and popular with families and retirees. San Miguel de Allende in the highlands and Lake Chapala with Ajijic near Guadalajara are also calm and well established. All three have large international communities and good medical care.

### What health insurance do I need in Mexico?
Legal residents can voluntarily enroll in the public IMSS system, with an age-based annual premium of roughly $450 to $1,200. Because of waiting periods, many pair IMSS with private insurance at around $520 to $1,230 per person per year and pay cash for cheap routine visits.
