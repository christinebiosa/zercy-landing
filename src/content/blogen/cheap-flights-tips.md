You know the feeling: you look at a flight, wait a day to decide — and the price goes up. Or down. Booking flights can feel like pure luck.

But there are patterns. And knowing them means paying less, systematically.

## 1. Tuesday and Wednesday are the cheapest days to book

Airlines fill their planes gradually. Weekends are when most people shop for tickets — so prices rise. Tuesdays and Wednesdays see the lowest booking volumes. That directly affects the price.

Not a secret anymore, but still true.

## 2. Book early — but not always

The rule of thumb: book European routes 6–8 weeks out, long-haul 3–6 months out. But watch out: airlines sell the first seats cheap, then raise prices, then drop them again if seats remain empty.

The sweet spot is rarely the day booking opens.

## 3. Flexible dates = up to 40% cheaper

"Sometime in October" is more expensive than "exactly October 14th." But "between October 8th and 22nd" can be 40% cheaper than your original target date.

## 4. Check nearby airports

Heathrow is more expensive than Stansted. Amsterdam is more expensive than Eindhoven. Vienna is more expensive than Bratislava.

Sounds obvious. But many travelers miss cheap options by only entering "their" airport. The travel time difference is often just 45 minutes — the price difference can be €120.

## 5. One-way can be cheaper than return

Airlines don't always price return trips as a unit. Sometimes it's cheaper to book the outbound with Airline A and the return with Airline B — or even split the same route across two bookings.

Downside: no joint baggage transfer, no protection if one leg is delayed.

## 6. Incognito mode helps — sometimes

The legend: airlines raise prices when they recognize your browser cookie. The reality: it happens, but less often than claimed. Doesn't hurt to search in incognito — occasionally helps.

What definitely helps: different devices, VPN from other countries, comparing multiple platforms.

## 7. Early morning flights save money

5:30 AM flights are unpopular. That drives the price down. Travelers willing to wake up early often pay significantly less — especially on busy routes like London–Barcelona or Amsterdam–Rome.
