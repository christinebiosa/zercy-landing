**Short answer:** For most US and UK trips through Europe, **AutoEurope** is our overall winner because it balances price, transparent final cost, and a support line you can actually reach. Heading to the Balkans, Georgia, or a Greek island, take **Localrent** (often no credit card hold and a low deposit). If you just want the rock-bottom price fast, **Economybookings** is strong, but read the insurance fine print first.

Five names show up on every car rental search. They all claim to be the cheapest. But they work in completely different ways under the hood, and that is what decides whether your trip runs smoothly or falls apart at the counter. Here is the honest comparison, no marketing spin.

## Broker, agent, or aggregator: what is the difference?

None of these platforms owns cars. But they sit at different points in the chain, and that matters more than the price on the homepage.

A **broker** sells you its own contract with its own insurance and handles claims itself. [Economybookings](https://www.economybookings.com/) works this way. The price is often very low, but a lot depends on the local supplier on the ground.

An **established agent** like [AutoEurope](https://www.autoeurope.com/) has sat between you and the big chains for over 60 years. It negotiates volume rates and bundles support in one place.

A **marketplace for local suppliers** like [Localrent](https://localrent.com/) surfaces small, often family-run rental firms you would never find otherwise. Those firms carry the better terms in regions like the Balkans.

An **aggregator** like [QEEQ](https://www.qeeq.com/) or [GetRentacar](https://getrentacar.com/) is a search engine. It gathers offers from many suppliers and forwards you along. The contract then runs through the supplier, not the platform. Once you can tell the models apart, you instantly know who to call when something goes wrong. The Wikipedia overview of [car rental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Car_rental) and its note on deposits and the damage waiver spells out the same logic.

## Is Economybookings actually any good?

Economybookings has run since 2008 and holds over 140,000 Trustpilot reviews averaging around 4.4 out of 5. Its prices sit regularly among the lowest in the market, especially for standard classes at major airports.

The catch is the insurance. The Economybookings Full Coverage is a reimbursement model: the local supplier still blocks a deposit of often 1,000 to 1,500 euros on your credit card at the counter. If damage happens, you pay first and then file a claim with Economybookings. Payouts do arrive according to user reports, but they typically take two to six weeks. If you budget tight or lack a big credit card buffer, plan for that. Whether the extra coverage is even worth buying, we break down in [Rental car insurance: is it worth it](/en/blog/rental-car-insurance-worth-it/).

## AutoEurope and Localrent: established versus local

AutoEurope is the steady all-rounder. UK consumer body Which? gave the agent an overall score of 79 percent in early 2026 and full marks for the clarity of its final cost. That is the core strength: you see the true total, no surprise at the end. The weak spot, per reviews, is limited support hours. As a pure agent, AutoEurope can only do so much in an on-site dispute. For classic European destinations, the UK, and fly-and-drive routes, it stays the most reliable pick.

Localrent plays a different card. The platform bundles local suppliers across more than 50 countries, strongest in Turkey, Greece, Georgia, Montenegro, Albania, and Cyprus. The average deposit sits around 300 euros, and many suppliers skip it entirely or take cash. That saves you when your credit card cannot absorb a 1,200-euro hold. Communication and handover often feel more personal than at the big chains, according to reviews.

## QEEQ and GetRentacar: the aggregators reviewed

QEEQ (formerly EasyRentCars, renamed in 2020) is a mature search engine with partners in over 200 countries. The upside: you compare Hertz, Avis, Europcar, Sixt, and local brands on one page. Prices are competitive, reviews are mixed. On support you often end up stuck between platform and supplier, because the contract lives with the supplier.

GetRentacar covers roughly 100 countries by its own count and lists many small suppliers. That is both the opportunity and the risk. Scam Detector gives the site a medium trust score of 66 out of 100, and common complaints involve last-minute supplier swaps with added fees. Use GetRentacar as a second opinion for a price check, but vet the specific supplier before you book. ## What should you watch for with deposits and insurance?

The homepage price is only half the truth. Three points decide the real cost.

**Deposit and credit card hold.** Almost every supplier blocks money on your credit card at pickup, often four figures. Debit cards get rejected a lot. On broker bookings the hold applies even with coverage bought. Only local suppliers via Localrent regularly skip it.

**Counter upsell on collision coverage.** The classic move: you bought Full Coverage online, and the counter still pushes an expensive add-on, sometimes with gentle pressure. Stick to your online coverage and ask to see the contract. More on this in [Rental car hidden costs to avoid](/en/blog/rental-car-hidden-costs-avoid/).

**Airport fees.** Picking up right at the terminal almost always adds 10 to 25 percent. A branch a few miles away with a shuttle is often clearly cheaper. All five platforms show this in the search. Before you drive off, the list in the [Rental car checklist](/en/blog/rental-car-checklist/) helps.

## Which platform for whom?

Short and honest, so you do not have to guess:

- **Europe, UK, reliable support:** AutoEurope. Transparent final cost beats the last dollar saved.
- **Balkans, Georgia, islands, low deposit stress:** Localrent. Low deposit, cash often accepted.
- **Absolute lowest price, you read the fine print:** Economybookings. Budget for the deposit hold.
- **Broad price check of the big chains:** QEEQ. Solid search engine, support via the supplier.
- **Second opinion and niche suppliers:** GetRentacar. Always vet the specific supplier.

## Where should you book in the end?

There is no single platform for everything, and anyone who says so is selling you something. Our flow: start with AutoEurope for a serious reference price. On Balkan or island destinations, also check Localrent for the deposit. Glance at Economybookings and QEEQ for the low price, but only if the insurance and deposit hold line up cleanly. That way you pair the best price with the calmest peace of mind.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Which car rental platform is cheapest in 2026?

On raw starting prices, Economybookings and QEEQ usually lead, especially for standard classes at major airports. The true final price can differ once the deposit hold and insurance are counted. So always compare the total price including your desired coverage, not just the first number shown.

### When is Localrent better than a big agent?

Localrent wins when you travel in regions like the Balkans, Georgia, Turkey, or the Greek islands. There, local suppliers offer a low deposit or none at all and often accept cash. That spares you the high credit card hold that the big platforms almost always require.

### Why does the supplier block a deposit despite the insurance I bought?

Because a broker like Economybookings uses a reimbursement model. The local supplier protects itself with its own deposit hold on your credit card. If damage occurs, you pay first and get the money back from the broker later, often after two to six weeks.

### How do you avoid the collision coverage upsell at the counter?

Buy your preferred coverage online and save or print the receipt. At the counter, decline add-ons politely and ask to see what the contract already includes. Check the fuel level and vehicle condition before you drive off so there are no disputes later.
