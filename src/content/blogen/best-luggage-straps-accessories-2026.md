Your suitcase looks exactly like everyone else's at baggage claim. Black hard-shell, no markings, nothing to tell it apart. Then someone else walks off with it. A few euros worth of accessories prevents that, and a lot more besides.

Luggage straps, TSA locks, bag tags, trackers, protective covers: travelers who start using them rarely go back. These seven products deserve a spot in your packing routine in 2026. Not all at once. But at least two.

## What should you look for?

Not every product delivers what the listing promises. Four criteria separate the useful from the useless.

**Adjustability:** A strap needs to fit different suitcase sizes. Look for an adjustment range of at least 100 to 200 cm. Narrower straps are easier to tighten but hold up less well under baggage-handling pressure.

**TSA compatibility:** In the US and Canada, the TSA is authorized to open checked baggage without notice. TSA-approved locks are opened with a master key and re-locked afterward. A non-TSA lock gets cut off, or disappears entirely.

**Visibility:** A bright color costs nothing extra. Spotting your bag in ten seconds at the carousel saves time and stress.

**Material:** Polyester straps stretch less than nylon and hold up better in humid conditions. For TSA locks, zinc alloy shells outlast pure plastic by a wide margin.

## Do you really need a luggage strap?

Yes. But not for the reason most assume. Straps rarely prevent a zipper from giving way. They serve three other purposes.

First: recognition. A colored band makes your bag instantly unmistakable. No more circling the carousel.

Second: deterrence. A bag with a visible strap and lock is a less attractive target. A visible barrier reduces the risk.

Third: tamper awareness. Some airlines open bags for inspection. A distinctive strap tells you at a glance whether anything has been moved.

## How do you make your luggage recognizable?

The simplest method: a brightly colored strap. Neon yellow, orange, or teal stand out. Black is a poor choice. It reads no differently from the bag itself.

Bag tags add a second layer. Name and phone number are enough. Full addresses on the outside are a bad idea. Put the card inside and show only your phone number on the exterior.

Stickers work, but peel after a few trips. A luggage cover is more durable and protects the surface at the same time.

Trackers like the Apple AirTag add a digital layer. You know where your bag is at any moment. That matters most on connecting flights or when an airline confirms your bag was checked but hasn't shown up yet. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does a good luggage strap cost?
Basic straps without a lock start around $6. Models with a TSA combination lock run $14 to $22. For a set with strap, lock, and tag, budget $25 to $45.

### Which trackers work without an iPhone?
Apple AirTags require iOS. Android users are better served by Tile Mate or Samsung SmartTag Plus. Both use device networks to locate a tracker. Coverage is slightly less than AirTag's but sufficient for most routes.

### How do you attach a luggage strap correctly?
Run the strap diagonally across the suitcase, not just around the wide face. It stays in place even when the bag is on its side. Position the buckle on a flat surface, not an edge. Pull tight until it no longer shifts.

### Why do you need a TSA lock when traveling to the US?
The TSA is authorized to open checked baggage without notice. A standard lock gets cut off or damaged. TSA-approved locks have a mechanism that agents open with a master key and re-lock afterward. No damage, no replacement needed.
