**Short answer:** Spain abolished the Golden Visa in April 2025. For Americans and Brits, three realistic routes remain: the Non-Lucrative Visa (NLV) for retirees and people with passive income, the Digital Nomad Visa for remote work, and the work or self-employment visa. Budget roughly 1,400 to 1,800 euros a month for living costs, depending on the city.

Sun, sea, slower pace, better food, and a lower cost of living than most of the US or UK. The dream of living in Spain is real and reachable. But the easiest door closed in 2025. If you were planning to buy a property and get residency in return, that option is gone.

This guide walks you through how it actually works in 2026. Real numbers, no fluff. By the end you will know which route fits you and what your first weeks on the ground will cost.

## What are the routes into Spain in 2026?

If you are not an EU citizen, you need a visa to live in Spain long term. That applies to US and UK nationals, since the UK left the EU. The good news: Spain has clear, well-trodden residency routes and a big expat community to learn from.

The Golden Visa is gone. Organic Law 1/2025 took effect on 3 April 2025 and repealed the old investor visa. The government blamed housing pressure: investments from 500,000 euros were pushing up property prices in popular cities. Anyone who already held a Golden Visa keeps it, including renewal rights. New applicants cannot use it.

That leaves three main paths, each with its own income test and rules. Choose based on how you earn your money, not on which sounds nicest.

## Which visa is right for you?

Use this decision grid:

- **Take the Non-Lucrative Visa (NLV) if** you live off passive income, pensions, or savings and will not work in Spain. In 2026 the main applicant must show around 28,800 euros a year, roughly 2,400 euros a month. Each family member adds about 7,200 euros a year. Working is not allowed on this visa.
- **Take the Digital Nomad Visa if** you work remotely for an employer or clients outside Spain. The 2026 income threshold sits around 2,849 euros a month for one person, tied to the Spanish minimum wage. At least 80 percent of your income should come from non-Spanish sources.
- **Take the work or self-employment visa if** you plan to be hired by a Spanish company or start a business locally. This is the most paperwork-heavy route, needing a contract or business plan.

If remote work in the sun is your plan, the Digital Nomad Visa is often the sweet spot. Compare Spain against other options in our roundup of [countries with a digital nomad visa](/en/blog/digital-nomad-visa-countries-2026/) before you commit.

## What does living in Spain actually cost?

Spain is cheaper than the US or UK, but no longer a bargain everywhere. Rents have climbed hard lately, up 3 to 8 percent a year in big cities.

Rough guides for one person, including rent:

- **Madrid:** from about 1,750 euros a month, often more. Central flats are pricey.
- **Barcelona:** similar to Madrid, usually at the top end.
- **Valencia:** around 1,650 euros, with a one-bedroom flat at 500 to 800 euros. About 15 to 20 percent cheaper than Madrid.
- **Malaga:** around 1,400 euros, small flats from 350 to 600 euros.

Groceries, restaurants, and public transport are noticeably cheaper than back home. The menu del dia, a multi-course lunch, often costs 12 to 15 euros. A US pension or a remote salary stretches a long way in a mid-sized coastal city. Your choice of city shapes your budget and daily life more than the visa does.

**Valencia** is many people's pick for best balance. Beach, culture, walkable size, fair prices. It draws expats without Barcelona's cost. While you hunt for a flat, our guide on [where to stay in Valencia](/en/blog/where-to-stay-valencia/) points you to the right base.

## Public healthcare or private insurance?

Spain's healthcare is among the best in Europe. Your access depends on your status.

If you work in Spain and pay social security, you are covered by the public system. Some retirees use forms from their home country to link coverage back home.

Everyone else can join the Convenio Especial, a voluntary pay-in to the public system. In 2026 it costs around 60 euros a month if you are under 65, and about 157 euros if you are older. You need twelve months of continuous town-hall registration first. The catch: you pay the full cost of prescription medicines yourself.

For visa applications, both NLV and Digital Nomad, full private health insurance valid in Spain is mandatory. Many expats keep the private plan afterward for shorter waiting times. You can check the official rules through Spain's [Ministry of Foreign Affairs](https://www.exteriores.gob.es/en/Paginas/index.aspx).

## The first weeks: NIE, padron, and a bank account

Spanish bureaucracy tests your patience. You will almost always need these three steps, in this order:

1. **Get your NIE.** The Numero de Identidad de Extranjero is your foreigner tax and ID number. Almost nothing works without it: no lease, no bank account, no phone contract. Book an appointment at the police or consulate.
2. **Register at the town hall (empadronamiento).** The certificate proves your local address and is required for healthcare, car registration, and more.
3. **Open a bank account.** With your NIE and town-hall certificate. Some banks accept non-residents at higher fees.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much money do I need for the Non-Lucrative Visa in 2026?
The main applicant must show around 28,800 euros a year, roughly 2,400 euros a month. Each additional family member adds about 7,200 euros a year. The amount is tied to Spain's IPREM reference figure and stayed stable for 2026.

### Why did Spain abolish the Golden Visa?
The government wanted to ease housing pressure. Investments from 500,000 euros were driving up property prices in popular cities. The program ended on 3 April 2025, though existing holders keep their rights.

### Which Spanish city is cheapest for expats?
Among the popular destinations, Malaga is often the most affordable larger coastal city, at around 1,400 euros a month for living costs. Valencia is slightly higher and seen as the best balance of price, beach, and city life. Madrid and Barcelona are noticeably pricier.

### How do expats access the Spanish healthcare system?
If you work and pay social security, you are covered automatically. Retirees often use a home-country form. Everyone else can join the Convenio Especial after twelve months of registration, for around 60 euros a month under 65. Visa applicants also need full private insurance.
